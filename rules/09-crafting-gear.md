# Crafting & Gear

## Crafting

Every item that is not Magical or Alchemical is considered mundane. Magical
and Alchemical permanent items take 4 days to craft, and consumables take a
minimum of 2 days to craft for a batch of one or two, plus 1 day for
additional units up to a maximum of 4 batches. It takes 2 days to craft a
mundane item, or 1 day or less if the item is not Mechanical and is
particularly simple to make (up to the GM's discretion). These items are
always shoddy unless you critically succeed on the Crafting check or are a
master in Crafting.

Formulas are required to craft Magical and Alchemical items, regardless of the
item's rarity. Formulas for mundane items are not necessary and halve the time
needed to create the item (from 2 to 1 day, from 1 day to 6 hours of
downtime).

Such crafting uses prepared parts as materials and only covers the assembly of
the final item. Making a complex item from raw, unrefined materials (such as
constructing a whole plate armor from iron ore) may take weeks of downtime and
use different rules.

## Day of Work

Unless the GM specifies otherwise, a day of Crafting requires 8 hours of work,
7 hours of continuous work, and 1 hour broken into multiple rests. The
character can undertake other activities in their free time (typically about 8
hours of wake time), but not another 8-hour taxing downtime such as Crafting
or Earning an Income.

## Improvised Materials

Materials used for Magical, Alchemical, and Mechanical crafting are typically
purchased from the open market. Remember that those are prepared parts instead
of raw materials, and you must only apply assembly during crafting. The GM may
allow you to replace purchased materials with raw materials gathered from
nature, usually by applying the Earn an Income rules using skill checks like
Crafting, Medicine, Nature, or applicable Lore. If the GM allows you to
improvise with raw materials, you must be at least an expert in Crafting. The
Crafting check uses a Hard DC adjustment. If the materials are of low quality,
particularly scarce, and ingenuity is needed to make it work, you must be at
least a master in Crafting, and the Crafting check uses a Very Hard DC
adjustment.

## Rushed Crafting

Crafting items assumes that the character will work 8 hours a day to produce
the desired item. Alternatively, the character can rush the crafting process by
working longer hours per day, taking fewer and shorter rests, and taking fewer
precautions during the process.

You can cut the time to craft from 4 days to 2 days by increasing the number
of hours worked in a day to 16 hours and increasing the DC by 2. However, if
you fail, you get a critical failure instead. Additional days spent during
Crafting to lower the item cost must be completed on time. You gain the
Fatigued condition at the end of the daily crafting activity.

## Crafting Together

Multiple creatures can contribute towards completing a single crafting process,
and four days can be distributed among them as they see fit. Each creature
that wishes to contribute at least one day of crafting must be able to craft
such items, and at least one of the creatures must have the formula for the
item. Each creature must attempt the Crafting check at the same DC as the
primary crafter. Among all crafting creatures, more than half of them should
succeed for the crafting to be successful (so two out of two must succeed, or
two out of three must succeed), and if any creature gets a critical failure,
the crafting is a critical failure. If the crafting is successful, each
contributing creature that succeeded on the Crafting check can spend additional
days to lower the cost of additional raw materials.

Alternatively, a creature may instead opt to Aid one crafting ally during a
crafting process with a skill other than Crafting. The aiding creature doesn't
need to be able to craft such items or need to know the formula to do so.
Instead of contributing days of its completion, that creature must make an Aid
attempt using an applicable skill against the Crafting DC of the item. The GM
may allow any relevant skills such as Arcana, Nature, Occultism and Religion
for crafting magical items, or an applicable Lore. If no skills apply, the
aiding Creature can always use Perception to Aid, but such attempts cannot
succeed critically.

## Adventuring Gear Changes

### Ration | ITEM 0

The Bulk of 7-days worth of Rations is changed to 2 Bulk, instead of Light.
Usually, characters carry one day's worth of Ration that weighs Light bulk.
The rest are stashed safely but tracked on the character sheet to easily
manage their remaining uses.

### Shield | ITEM 1

All shields without speed penalty have the shove trait but can shove only
creatures of your size or lower.

### Tower Shield | ITEM 1

When you Raise a Shield using a tower shield, you provide standard cover
instead of lesser cover against ranged attacks that pass through your space. Up
to one ally adjacent to you can use the Take Cover action to benefit from
standard cover. At the same time, the shield is raised and you remain adjacent
to it and can upgrade it to greater cover with an additional Take Cover action.

## Poisons

Poisons will use the statistics and rules from the legacy PF2 Core Rulebook and
pre-Remaster supplements. Player Characters (such as Toxicologists) can craft
poisons using the same sources.

### Poison Application

You can use a dose of poison with the Injury trait and apply its contents to
four pieces of ammunition (such as arrows or bolts), two thrown weapons (such
as daggers), or one melee weapon. A dose of poison can be used entirely at once
or used half. When half used, the remainder can be applied to half the weapons
of a specific type, rounded down.

The application of poison on weapons usually happens outside of combat and
requires careful handling. In battle, you must use both your hands to hold and
apply poison to your weapons. It takes ◆ Interact action to pull out your
poison with one hand, ◆ Interact actions to apply on each weapon or
ammunition. You can spread these actions across multiple turns as long as the
vial of poison is not disarmed from your hand. Feats may allow you faster
application of poisons to a single weapon or ammunition.

> **Why the Change?** The core rules treat poisons as a single-use consumable
> on one melee weapon or ammunition. This dramatically discourages their use
> for projectiles as ammunition that misses is wasted while melee weapon
> retains its poison until it hits or the poison dries. This change allows more
> uses on types of weapons that are considered expended after one attack. The
> rule finds ammunition as expended weapons, thrown weapons as temporarily
> expended weapons until recovered from the ground, and melee weapons as
> persistent weapons.

## Snares

Snare items with mechanical traits crafted using raw materials become broken
once set and activated. Broken Snares can be repaired and reused with the
Repair Snare, a trained Crafting exploration activity.

### Repair Snare | TRAINED CRAFTING ACTIVITY

[EXPLORATION] [MANIPULATE]
**Requirements:** You have a repair kit (page 291) and are trained in
Crafting.
**Target** one Snare with the mechanical or alchemical trait, that was
manufactured using the Crafting downtime activity using raw materials and is
broken after use

You spend 10 minutes attempting to fix a used but not wholly destroyed
mechanical Snare, exchanging damaged components for new ones. You must also
spend one-tenth of the Snare cost in repair material before attempting to
repair if the trap is mechanical, or one-fifth if it is alchemical. The GM
sets the DC, but it’s usually about the same DC to Repair a Snare as it is to
Craft it in the first place.

If the cost of repair materials is 150 gp or higher, the time of repair
increases to 1 hour. If the cost is 300 gp or higher, the time of repair
increases to 4 hours of work. If the cost is 500 gp or higher, the time to
repair increases to 1 downtime day.

**Critical Success** As Success, you recover half of the repair material cost
you spent during the repairs.

**Success** You restore the Snare and it can be used again. Once used, it
becomes broken and can be repaired again.

**Failure** You fail to restore the Snare, but the cost of repair materials is
recovered.

**Critical Failure** You destroy the Snare entirely in your futile attempt to
repair it. The cost of repairing materials is also lost.

**Special** Feats that improve the Repair activity also apply to the Repair
Snare activity. Feats that change the activity duration apply only as long as
the duration is not increased due to Snare repair costs.

> **Why the Change?** Snares purchased on the market or crafted with raw
> materials are considered overpriced for their limited effect. As a consumable
> broken with its setup, they make an inferior investment even if it is never
> triggered because the enemy spotted and avoided them. Only Snares made by
> classes or archetypes as part of their daily preparation are considered
> worthwhile because they are technically free. These rules for resetting and
> repairing a broken Snare improve their usability: twice the price of a Snare
> allows for up to 11 uses through ongoing maintenance.
