# General Changes

## Hero Points

Players start a new campaign with two Hero Points each. Hero points are not
lost between sessions. In addition to feats of heroism and rewards for quality
roleplaying, players earn Hero Points in the following ways:

- Players earn a Hero Point at the end of a game session.
- Players earn a Hero Point after overcoming a Severe or Extreme difficulty
  encounter.

Hero Points can reroll only checks where you see the result, and that excludes
secret checks.

## Insight Checks

At any point, you can attempt a DC 13 flat check. If you succeed, you can pass
a suggestion to the target player that his character may recall in the game as
a spark of insight or recalled memory. If you fail the check, you may not
attempt another such check until the end of the scene. You can do this even if
your character is absent from the scene or has been killed.

> **The type of check using Wisdom, inspired by 5E campaigns I ran in the
> past, has been removed in favor of a simple 40% flat check.**

## Social Checks and Roleplaying

*Pathfinder 2nd Edition* has strict rules for making checks for social
activities such as intimidating or pressing others (via Coerce), manipulating
others to do what you want (Lie, Make an Impression, and Request), collecting
information (via Gather Information), and more. Experienced players from casual
or rules-lite games are used to making these activities without checks and only
with engagement in actual roleplay. They might consider GM calls to make such
checks as unwanted discouragement to roleplay. At the same time, ignoring the
checks and all the feats that improve them removes a big part of the Pathfinder
tactical depth outside combat.

To recognize both approaches of roleplay as valid, the GM will usually call for
a roll for social roleplaying activities when the players want to resolve it
quickly without roleplaying it in depth and just summarizing the result when
there is significant resistance or hostility from the creature, or the player
wants to make the roll for an additional benefit. The player’s engagement in
roleplaying the activity, as long as it is not detrimental to the desired
result, provides up to +2 untyped bonus to the check. A great and inspired
roleplaying of the activity or creative approach to resolving it offers up to a
 +4 untyped bonus to the check.

The creature's disposition towards the party or specific members modifies the
checks. Creatures that are indifferent or have better dispositions may increase
the difficulty to hardness (DC+2) if they are forced to do things they don't
wish to do. Unfriendly creatures always increase the check’s difficulty to hard
(DC+2), and hostile creatures increase the difficulty to very hard (DC+5) if
the check can be made.

## Movement in Dangerous Areas

All players are assumed to move at half speed in a dangerous environment (such
as exploring a dungeon or an enemy camp) while out of the initiative. This
allows every character to perform one of the half-speed movement exploration
activities best suited for their class or party role (such as Avoid Notice,
Cover Tracks, Defend, Detect Magic, Investigate, Scout, or Track). Players need
to announce what action they will perform by default only once when entering
the area, and it will apply until they make a different choice. The party moves
at full speed without performing Exploration actions, but only if they announce
they do.

Also, as a part of the announcement of default actions, the party should decide
on its front, middle, and back positions in the party order for situations
where the party moves through tight corridors. This is useful for the GM when
deciding how to place characters on the map when an encounter starts.

## Creature Identification

When you succeed at Recall Knowledge about a creature, you learn its name,
traits, and a little bit about its history or behavior as part of the creature's
common lore. You can ask any question related to it or any of the questions
related to its statistics listed below. If you beat the DC by 2 or more, you
ask two questions; if you exceed the DC by 5 or more, you can ask three
questions; and if you beat the DC by 10 or more, you receive an answer on every
question you might have.

- Is the creature more powerful than you (higher level) or not (equal or lesser
  level)?
- What is the weakest saving throw out of all three?
- What is the creature's most vital skill, and does it have any special bonuses
  from specific circumstances?
- What is the creature's best-known passive ability?
- What are the creature's most used special attack, damage type, and traits?
- What is the creature's best-known active ability?
- What is the creature's highest level innate spell it can cast?

If a creature has multiple abilities of the same type, such as more than one
passive ability, the GM will let you know that you can try to remember more.
This enables you to use any unspent question or further attempts to Recall
Knowledge to learn the next best-known item of the same type. The GM will also
let you know if you have exhausted everything you can know about the creature
so you don't waste further attempts.

If a creature has an Uncommon or Rare rarity or a higher level because it is a
modified version of a Common rarity creature of the same or lower level (such
as an Elite adjustment), an attempt to Recall Knowledge about it may also
succeed if it beats the DC based on the level and rarity of the Common rarity
base creature. Such success only provides information about the Common rarity
base creature and does not reveal the unique abilities or statistical changes
of the Uncommon or Rare variant.

Each subsequent attempt to Recall Knowledge using the same skill increases the
check’s difficulty by one step (up to incredibly hard at +10 to DC). If you can
use a different skill that applies to the creature and has not used it before,
you do not increase the difficulty by one step when you switch to that skill
for the next check. During combat, when you spend ◆ to Recall Knowledge that
fails, you can still attempt to Recall Knowledge again as long as you don't
critically fail. Outside of combat, failing at Recall Knowledge works as usual.

### Abilities that trigger Recall Knowledge

Suppose you have abilities that require you to attempt a Recall Knowledge check
to get a non-information benefit. In that case, you may make that check without
learning anything about the creature using the first attempt DC unmodified by
difficulty due to multiple attempts. This check does not count towards your
multiple attempts to get answers to questions regarding the creature and does
not prevent you from making the check again if it is a failed check. This
check is not a successful Recall Knowledge for other feat requirements.

> To properly apply this rule, the player should call “I want to Recall
> Knowledge to learn about” for normal Recall Knowledge with difficulty
> modifiers for each attempt and hard failure, but call “I want to Recall
> Knowledge to use my `Action or Feat Name`” to ignore the difficulty modifier
> and hard failure and get no information on success. However, they get the
> effect of the Action or Feat they use.

## Access to Summoned Creatures

Using *summon* spells and rituals, you can summon creatures available in the
following books as they represent common knowledge for most characters:

- Bestiary 1, 2, 3; Monster Core
- Any other Pathfinder2e book that contains the rules for your class (i.e., if
  your class comes from Book of the Dead, the undead creatures contained in this
  book are common knowledge to you)

If you want to summon a creature outside of these sources or a creature with a
rarity other than common, you must make a hard Recall Knowledge (DC+2) based on
the creature's level, only once per unique creature. You can retry the Recall
Knowledge once you gain a new level; its difficulty increases with each
following attempt as normal. If you succeed, you recollect that you have
observed or studied the creature in detail and can summon it from now on with
no further checks. If you fail, you do not know the creature and need to find a
specimen to observe or study its anatomy or a source of research that details
its physiology and behavior in detail (using indirect sources of information
increases the difficulty to very hard, DC+5).

## Sickened Out of Combat

Conditions such as *sickened* that require action during combat to decrease its
value will automatically expire if the target spends 1 minute performing the
required effort and has more than a 5 percent chance to succeed per roll (such
as success if they roll 19 on a d20). This removes the need to make a lot of
unnecessary rolls when timing is not essential.

## Less Restrictive Fatigue

While you have the Fatigued condition, you can still use exploration activities
performed while traveling. However, you suffer a -4 status penalty for all
exploration activity checks and cannot forgo the check with an assurance feat.
With GM's approval, a fatigued character can take twice the time (or twice the
travel actions during a Hexploration) needed to complete the exploration
activity to lower the penalty to -2.

If another effect makes you Fatigued while you are already Fatigued, you stop
being able to use exploration activities until you recover from fatigue after a
full night's rest.

## Repeating Force Open

If a character fails at a Force Open check to break a door or a container
outside of combat, they fail to open it on the first try within a few seconds.
The DM may grant the character an automatic success if they commit to 30
seconds of uninterrupted Force Open activity. If the player needs to roll 15 or
more to succeed, the DM may increase the duration of the activity to 1 minute;
if they need 18 or more the duration may be increased up to 5 minutes; if they
need to roll 20 to succeed the duration may be increased up to 10 minutes.
After the activity duration ends, the character gets the success result but
cannot get the critical success result. During the activity, the character
generates noise of breaking and cracking and may attract the attention of
hostile creatures.

## Throwing at an Open Space

Throwing an object within a space you can see rather than at a creature
requires a ranged attack roll against a DC, with a range increment of 10 feet
and no limit of total increments. The DC is equal to 5 for throwing anywhere
within the square, DC 15 for throwing at half of the square, DC 25 for hitting
a tiny portion of the square no smaller than five inches (such as into a large
jar), and DC 35 for hitting a slit or a crack that can be no smaller than an
inch. If creatures or objects occupy the square, items thrown this way only
deal splash damage. If you fail, the object falls as far and as accurately as
your roll totals allow.

## Alternative Ways to Disable Hazards

Many Hazards allow for multiple skills to disable them, however, these skills
are not automatically known by the players. Additionally, Hazards without a
clear physical or mechanical way of disabling may not allow for the default
use of Thievery skill.

As long as you observe the Hazard, you can attempt to Recall Knowledge about
the hazard. The skill depends on the type of hazard: mechanical hazards such as
snares and traps use Crafting or Thievery; environmental hazards use Nature or
Survival; magical hazards use Arcana; haunts use Religion or Occultism. On a
success, the GM will reveal those options. If the Hazard can be disabled with a
spell use, the GM reveals those options if you are at least trained in the
skill related to the spell's tradition.

## Gentler Death Effects

If you fail a saving throw against an effect with the death trait, you can use
any number of Hero Points to reroll the saving throw instead of only once.

> Gentle Massive Damage has been removed from the house rules. It has been
> added to the Diehard general feat to improve its limited usability.

## Simplified Familiar Presence

Many groups treat familiars as quantum creatures because they lack easy rules
to manage their presence - they are not present during danger, they appear
when needed even during combat. To avoid such ambiguity these rules simplify
the management of familiars to avoid such metagaming without adding the
complexity of managing one more creature on the combat map.

At any moment of exploration outside combat, the master can choose one of
three modes for their familiar presence during combat - Reach, Near, and Far.
It is suggested that one mode is selected by default. Unless the master is
Surprised or Distracted when rolling for initiative, they can instruct their
familiar to switch to any other mode usually by hearing or gesture if there’s
line of sight. During combat, the master can spend ◆ Command action to make
their familiar switch to a different mode, and the familiar spends several
Stride actions as listed below.

**Reach** The familiar is either riding on the master's body, is hidden in
their clothing, or is hovering near them. The familiar is within touch
distance for the purpose of actions and spell effects that require touch,
unless they are inside a container. While the familiar is unlikely to be
targeted by attacks unless it or its master draws attention to the familiar,
they both suffer the effects of area damage that targets the master's space.
While in this mode, the master can use ◆ Command to instruct the familiar to
use a an interact action and enter a special container (such as Familiar
Satchel) or any other container (such as their backpack, but it costs ◆◆
interact to enter or leave a container not made to host a familiar creature).
While hidden inside a container, the familiar benefits from the material's
Hardness but lacks awareness and line of effect to interact with anything
outside the container. As long as the creature is in a container attached to
the body of another creature (such as its master) it does not roll separate
Reflex to avoid area of effect damage, it uses the carrying creature’s level
of success.

**Near** The familiar is instructed to stand by between 40 and 60 feet and use
whatever environment it can to cover against area effects. The familiar still
maintains a line of sight and effect with their master and the rest of the
combat. In this mode, the familiar is unlikely to suffer most of the area
damage effects unless the area is larger than 60 feet in radius. The familiar
must use two ◆ Stride actions to reach their master if commanded to switch to
Reach mode, which happens within the master's current turn.

**Far** The familiar is instructed to run away into a safe and explored nearby
environment where enemies cannot reach it, usually at 120 feet or more. The
familiar has no line of sight and effect at this distance but will respond to
◆ Command by hearing. The familiar needs to use four ◆ Stride actions to
reach its master if they are commanded to switch to Reach mode; this means
that it will appear at its master's disposal not immediately but at the start
of the master's next turn but cannot be commanded (take other actions other
than Stride to its master) during the master's next turn.

### Additional Familiar Uses

**Familiar Sacrifice** In desperate situations where the master is
unconscious or dying and their life depends on one hit deflected by any means a
familiar may be willing to sacrifice for their Master. Without the need of
Command, at the start of the master's turn, the familiar will appear near their
body (if in Reach mode) or Stride to do so (if in Near mode). Enemies who lack
the intelligence to recognize the futile attempt will direct their attacks
toward the familiar instead of finishing off the fallen master. The master can
also use 1A Command to ask the familiar to do the same for another fallen ally.

**Backpack Familiar** Familiars that hide in their master's backpack can take
◆ Command to ◆ Rummage for an item and ⟳ Pass to Ally towards their master.
The master can use their reaction to take the item passed by the familiar
using a free hand.
